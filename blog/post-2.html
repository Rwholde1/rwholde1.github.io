Realism in Game Design: Making Mechanics that Reflect Reality

When designing for Pharmini, one of the most important lessons I learned was that I needed to design mechanics that fit the game’s level of realism. Pharmini’s goal is to inspire interest in industrial engineering, which means that the game needs to work like a simulation in which players embody the profession. I fell short of this when designing a mechanic to rebalance the amount of money the player obtained throughout the game. In the late stages of the game, the player was able to accumulate and save too much money, which in turn meant that all other systems felt arbitrary. The player could build and subsequently max out a lab or factory without putting a dent in their savings once they were set up, which made the game lose replayability. In conjunction with the excessive amounts of money players could accumulate, a playtester had found a strategy for the game in which they placed many labs and factories to support each hospital in order to ensure that no lab was burdened with too many orders and were always supplied with materials. I felt this strategy went against the spirit of the game; I wanted players to have to carefully choose the positions of their labs and factories in order to support their hospitals, and the issues with money helped to jeopardize this by allowing players to earn more money than they needed. As such, I knew a change was necessary.

The goal was to make the placement of labs and factories feel more meaningful while simultaneously making building them more expensive, curbing the excessive amount of extra cash gathered by the end of the game. I approached the game design problem with a game design sensibility, and what I identified was that the prices of buildings were static throughout the game and very low once the late game was reached. This gave me the idea to exponentially increase the price of buildings based on how many the player had built, which would simultaneously make the price of buildings in the late game higher as well as place a “soft cap” on the number of buildings a player could place as the price became unreachable. I developed an equation, modeled it, and playtested with the mechanic implemented.

The Normal difficulty curve for exponential build price increase


The mechanical change was very successful, receiving positive feedback during playtesting and solving the issues that it needed to solve. Players were still earning a lot of money, but the money they were earning was being saved to be spent on a large purchase, not saved endlessly to make the game unloseable. The high costs in turn made it impossible to frivolously build to support one hospital: placing buildings thoughtfully was much more important. The mechanic solved the game design issues with Pharmini successfully.

However, the mechanic did not fit with the realistic simulation the game hoped to give players. Exponentially increasing cost is a very gamey mechanic. Companies don’t endure exponentially increasing costs on a factory because they own three others: the cost of property is based on its location and surrounding infrastructure. I had solved a realistic game’s problem with a solution that didn’t acknowledge that realistic aspect of the game. It was a very obvious flaw once someone else pointed it out, but I was so focused on solving the problem and the positive results of my solution. There are other aspects of Pharmini that are unrealistic: for example, a building costs $500 in game, which it obviously doesn’t in the real world. But exponential price increase is a model for the expansion of an industrial engineering company, so it needs to be realistic in order for the rest of the simulation to be cohesive.

This issue made me think a lot about the cross-section between fun and realism. In most first-person shooters, a health bar is used rather than forcing players to aim for vulnerable areas. The result is a more fun game due to this simplification. But Escape From Tarkov is more fun to some because it provides realism other games don’t, such as using the correct ammo types for guns or bandaging and healing appendages in certain ways. Realism increases the stakes of the game, making it more intense and more fun. But splinting a leg would be untenable in Call of Duty because it would break up the fast-paced action the games are known for poorly. Even more interesting is that both games require realism to a certain extent. Guns need to look, feel, and sound a certain way to create a combat experience. As such, knowing how realistic a game needs to be is important to how mechanics are designed and how they combine to create the game’s feel for the player.

With the identification of the unrealistic nature of exponential build price increase, I went back to the drawing board with the general idea from the lead designer of an operational cost for buildings. When designing for this problem, I was more conscious of the realism necessary for the game and focused on managing players’ money throughout the game rather than just at the end. Operating costs aided greatly in this focus, so I developed an equation for how much the player would need to pay for each of their buildings each round. I tied operating cost to a system of maintenance for buildings that required players to keep track of their operating cost in order to keep their buildings functional. I also designed a new system to increase the price of building new labs and factories which worked off of surrounding buildings and the current round rather than an exponential model.

These design changes solidified some of the last holes in the game’s design. The player’s money is now managed throughout the game, and it gave room for some interesting additions in the early game to increase engagement. This change also led to improved city generation by having a city be initially generated rather than only be generated with hospitals, making building cost more impactful. This design solution solved the problem better while also maintaining the level of realism the game needed. Understanding this level of realism is very important to designing a game correctly, and I’m glad to have learned this lesson and corrected my mistake.
